title: 'AI gave me my job back'
description: 'Using AI coding tools made me spend less time on boilerplate and more time on the engineering work I actually studied for.'

I've worked as a software engineer both before and after AI coding assistants became normal. The surprise for me wasn't that I felt less useful. It was the opposite: **I feel more like an engineer again.**

That probably sounds backwards. Most of the conversation right now is about AI replacing developers, automating programming, or turning engineering into prompting. My day-to-day experience has been almost the opposite.

The more AI helps with implementation, the more my role shifts toward the parts of the job that made me fall in love with the field in the first place: systems thinking, architecture, product collaboration, UX iteration, tradeoffs, and long-term maintainability.

Ironically, AI may be the thing that finally let me apply what I spent years studying.

## What I actually studied for

I have a master's in software engineering. A huge chunk of that education wasn't about memorizing syntax or grinding boilerplate. It was about systems: architecture, scalability, distributed systems, abstraction, maintainability, reliability, modeling, and managing complexity over time.

The goal was never to become someone who writes code fast. It was to become someone who can design and evolve systems responsibly.

Then I entered the industry and found out that modern software development often optimizes for implementation throughput. Even on good teams, an enormous amount of time goes to wiring components, CRUD flows, repetitive TypeScript errors, translating tickets into production code, and handling endless detail work.

That work matters. Products are built on it. But there's a difference between work that needs deep engineering judgment and work that mainly needs time and attention.

Before AI, even when I had a chance to influence architecture or product direction, most of my energy still went into implementation. That balance is changing.

## Production code is not suddenly free

I strongly disagree with the idea that AI made software development trivial.

Good production code is still hard. AI-generated code can become dangerous quickly if nobody reviews it thoughtfully or guides it properly.

AI is extremely good at generating **plausible** code. That is not the same as generating production-ready systems. Without engineering judgment, it can produce fragile abstractions, inconsistent architecture, hidden coupling, poor separation of concerns, and maintainability nightmares. And because it generates so fast, it can accelerate bad decisions just as fast as good ones.

What changed is that certain parts of implementation became faster to explore:

- prototyping and proof-of-concepts
- trying alternate approaches
- testing ideas quickly
- getting something into the browser fast enough for a real UX discussion

AI is incredible for iteration. Production engineering still requires humans making careful decisions. If anything, the responsibility on engineers went up, because we can now generate far more code than we can safely trust by default.

> The distinction that matters: AI speeds up exploration. It does not remove the need for judgment.

## Thinking beats typing

The biggest impact on my work is not that I type less. It's that implementation consumes less of my cognitive bandwidth.

So I spend more time on the actual problem: analyzing requirements, finding edge cases, questioning assumptions, shaping specs, evaluating tradeoffs, and talking about technical constraints early. That work used to compete with the sheer cost of implementation. Now I can slow down and think before building.

That shift feels healthier. A mediocre implementation of a good idea can often be improved later. A perfect implementation of the wrong solution is much more expensive.

## Product and UX got closer

One unexpected effect: collaborating outside traditional engineering boundaries got easier.

I can build functional prototypes extremely quickly. Not polished production systems. Not final architecture. But enough to validate ideas, test interactions, explore UX flows, expose technical limits, and help designers iterate.

That changes conversations. Instead of debating abstract ideas for days, I can often put something tangible in front of people almost immediately. Once stakeholders can interact with an idea, the discussion gets dramatically better.

Sometimes a concept that sounded great in a meeting feels awkward the moment it exists in a browser. Sometimes a tiny UX change unlocks a cleaner implementation. Sometimes engineering constraints reshape the product in a positive way before costly work begins.

AI shortened the distance between "idea" and "experience." Engineering shows up earlier in the process instead of only appearing after everything has already been decided.

## What changed outside the team

This shift wasn't only internal. It changed how non-engineers perceive the work too.

Before, a lot of engineering value was invisible. Stakeholders mainly saw output: features shipped, bugs fixed, tickets closed. Now the collaboration itself is more visible. Because engineers can prototype quickly and contribute earlier, product managers, designers, and leadership increasingly see engineering as part of shaping the solution, not just implementing it.

More conversations now involve feasibility, operational simplicity, maintenance cost, edge cases, scalability, and how the system behaves under real conditions. Less "here is the spec, please build it." More "let's figure out the right solution together."

For me, that has been one of the most fulfilling changes.

## Code review got more interesting

Reviews changed too. Before AI, a lot of review energy went to syntax, formatting, repetitive patterns, small refactors, naming. Those things still matter.

But the most valuable discussions now happen at a higher level:

- architectural consistency
- domain boundaries
- abstraction quality
- long-term readability
- operational implications
- failure scenarios

The conversation shifted from "does this code work?" to "is this the right way for the system to evolve?" AI-generated code often looks convincing very quickly. That means engineers need **stronger** judgment, not weaker.

## Architecture matters more than ever

When code generation accelerates, architecture becomes even more important. Because now you can create enormous amounts of complexity extremely quickly.

AI is very good at extending existing patterns locally. It is much less reliable at preserving long-term coherence across an entire system. That responsibility still belongs to engineers.

I now spend far more time thinking about boundaries, ownership, modularity, system evolution, dependency management, data flow, and what it will feel like to live with this codebase in five years, not five weeks.

Many of these are the exact concepts software engineering education tried to emphasize all along. Not syntax. Not boilerplate. Systems thinking. Generating code is only the beginning.

## The ceiling went up

There's a popular narrative that AI lowers the barrier to software development. I think that's true to some extent.

But it also raises the ceiling for good engineering. Once implementation accelerates, the differentiators become judgment, communication, architecture, product intuition, prioritization, system design, and understanding tradeoffs. The value shifts upward: from producing code to directing complexity responsibly.

That feels much closer to what engineering was always supposed to be.

## What I'm taking from this

The strangest part: AI did not reduce the importance of my engineering background. It amplified it.

For the first time in my career, a larger portion of what I spent years studying is directly relevant to my daily work. Not because AI replaced engineering, but because it removed enough mechanical friction for deeper engineering work to surface again.

I still write code. I still review implementations carefully. I still think production quality matters enormously. But now I spend more time shaping systems, guiding architecture, collaborating across disciplines, thinking long term, and solving product problems instead of only implementing solutions.

AI didn't turn me into a ticket processor. If anything, it pushed my role closer to what I imagined software engineering would be when I decided to study it.
